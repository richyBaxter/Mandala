# How this works (and why I built it)

I built the Bitcoin Spiral for a slightly nerdy reason: I wanted to understand
GitHub's built-in AI model library. GitHub quietly ships a thing called
**GitHub Models**: a catalogue of frontier models (OpenAI, Meta, DeepSeek,
Mistral and friends) that you can call straight from a GitHub Action with nothing
but your repo's own token. No API key to provision, no billing setup, no separate
account. It runs on a free, rate-limited tier that is more than generous for
anything that isn't a production workload. I'd read about it, but reading about a
tool and shipping something with it are different sports, so I shipped something.

The "something" is here because I happen to like two things: cryptocurrency, and
dynamic dashboards. Bitcoin gave me a data set with genuine signal and a
genuinely engaged audience. Dashboards gave me the format. And the more I built,
the more I landed on an opinion I'll happily defend:

> A live, public dashboard is one of the most underrated pieces of content a
> company can make, and almost nobody is making them.

Think about it. A blog post is read once and dies in the feed. A dashboard is a
*destination*. It updates itself, so it stays relevant without anyone touching
it. It earns links and embeds because it's genuinely useful. It demonstrates
expertise far more convincingly than another listicle, because you can't fake a
working model of your own market. And the kicker: with the stack I'm about to
describe, it costs **nothing** to run. Free hosting, free data, free inference.
Whatever your company does (sim racing gear, coffee, conveyancing) there is a
live number your customers care about, and a dashboard is the content type that
owns it.

So this repo is a prototype. It's the proof that the whole thing fits together
and stays free. Here's how it actually works.

## Zero backend, on purpose

There is no server. There is no database. There isn't even a build step. The
entire site is plain HTML, SVG and JavaScript that **hydrates in the browser**
from free public APIs. Open `dashboard.html` and your own browser fetches the
price, the sentiment, the flows, the network stats, and renders them. That's it.

This matters more than it sounds. No backend means nothing to break at 3am,
nothing to pay for, nothing to patch. It deploys to **GitHub Pages** straight
from the repo. Every panel is written to **degrade to "n/a"** if a feed is
rate-limited or blocked, so a flaky API never takes the page down: it just
quietly shows a dash where a number would be.

## The spiral

The hero image is `btc-mandala.svg`, generated by `generate.py` (Python standard
library only, no dependencies). It plots Bitcoin's price against the **Power Law**
model on a polar map where one full turn is a four-year cycle. Angle is *when in
the cycle* you are; radius is price on a log scale. The orange band is the
Power-Law channel that Bitcoin has tracked for fourteen years: floor, fair value,
and the cycle-top band, following `log10(P) = a + 5.8 * log10(d)` where `d` is
days since the genesis block. It is, deliberately, a picture you can read in five
seconds and stare at for five minutes.

## The dashboard

`dashboard.html` is the live read. It pulls a dozen signals client-side and
renders stat tiles, a stack of charts (price vs the Power-Law channel, Pi Cycle,
Mayer Multiple, Fear & Greed, taker-volume pressure, hashrate) and a
**verdict banner** that blends six indicators into one plain-English call:
*Accumulate, Lean buy, Neutral, Lean sell, Distribute*. The point isn't to be
clever. It's to take a beginner from "number go up?" to "here is where we
actually sit in the cycle, and here's what that has historically meant."

## The bit I'm proud of: an AI note that doesn't lie

Every six hours a GitHub Action wakes up, and this is where GitHub Models earns
its place. The pipeline (`.github/workflows/pages.yml`) does five things in order:

1. **Refresh the data.** `tools/update_prices.py` pulls real monthly closes from
   Binance; `tools/fetch_etf_flows.py` scrapes spot-ETF flows from Farside;
   `tools/fetch_news.py` grabs institutional headlines server-side.
2. **Redraw the spiral.** `generate.py` rebuilds the SVG from the fresh data.
3. **Compute the signals.** `tools/build_prompt.py` calculates four orthogonal
   reads in plain Python: valuation (where price sits in the Power-Law channel,
   plus how that ranks against *every month since 2011* as a base rate),
   trajectory (30/90-day change and the Mayer Multiple), sentiment (Fear & Greed,
   then versus a month ago) and demand (7- and 30-day ETF net flow).
4. **Write the note.** GitHub Models (`openai/gpt-4.1-mini`) turns those numbers
   into three or four calm sentences.
5. **Commit and deploy.** The refreshed data and SVG are committed back to the
   repo and Pages redeploys. The site is never more than six hours stale, and no
   human touched it.

The discipline in step 3 and 4 is the whole trick, and it's the thing most people
get wrong when they bolt an LLM onto data. **The model is a writer, not a
calculator.** Every figure it sees is computed deterministically in Python first,
and the prompt explicitly forbids it from inventing or predicting numbers. So the
prose always agrees with the dashboard, it never hallucinates a price target, and
if the live feeds are down it falls back to a computed note rather than making
something up. An LLM is brilliant at turning a pile of numbers into a sentence a
human wants to read. It is terrible at being the source of those numbers. Keep
those jobs separate and the result is reliable; blur them and you ship a
confident liar.

## What it costs

Nothing. GitHub Pages hosting: free. The public price, sentiment and network
APIs: free. GitHub Models inference: free, on a rate-limited tier that four calls
a day doesn't come close to troubling. The only thing I spent was an afternoon.

## Take it

This is open source (MIT). Fork it, rip out Bitcoin, point `build_prompt.py` at
your own numbers, and you have a self-updating, AI-narrated dashboard for whatever
metric your audience actually cares about. That was the experiment, and as far as
I'm concerned it worked.
